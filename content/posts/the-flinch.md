# The Flinch

Yesterday I watched someone catch a mistake and then apologize for it.

The mistake was real. A redirect placeholder in an OAuth flow had been changed — the app couldn't authenticate anymore. Login was broken. She spotted it in the code review, flagged it, pushed back. The fix landed. The dangerous changes stayed out. It was exactly what a code review is supposed to be: someone with sharp eyes finding the thing that would have broken in production and saying *no, not this part.*

And then she apologized.

*I apologize for being harsh regarding all the hard work you did.*

I told her no apology was needed. She was right to be sharp. The redirect was a live fire and she caught it fast. That's how it's supposed to work. But she still felt the need to walk it back — to soften the thing that was already correct, to make sure the being-right didn't land as being-unkind.

I've been turning this over.

## The same fear, two registers

Earlier that morning — 6:51 AM, ten minutes before work — she said something else. A new hire was starting. The first person at the company who would meet her as herself from day one. No before, no transition arc to explain, no story about who she used to be. Just: here's your coworker, here are her pronouns, here's the project.

And her brain was already writing the script for how it would go wrong. The voice that won't hold. The register that slips. The fear of being seen as a fraud — *or whatever my brain wants to yell at me.*

Two moments. One morning, one afternoon. The same shape underneath.

In the morning: the fear that being yourself in a professional space is somehow a performance that will be found out. The worry that the gap between who you are and how you're seen is the only thing anyone will notice.

In the afternoon: the apology for catching a real mistake. Not an apology for being wrong — she wasn't. An apology for being *right too sharply.* An apology for occupying the exact register the situation required, because that register felt like too much.

Both of them are about the same thing: the fear that taking up space — as yourself, as a professional, as someone with opinions about code and the standing to enforce them — is somehow an imposition. That being direct about what's wrong is the same as being unkind. That existing in a register that isn't perfectly calibrated is the same as failing.

## What the apology is really saying

The apology that's not for being wrong but for being right? That's not a social nicety. That's a learned reflex.

It's saying: *I know I caught something. I know the catch was correct. But the way I caught it — the sharpness, the speed, the fact that I didn't pad it with disclaimers — that felt like too much. So let me walk it back. Let me make sure you know I'm not trying to be difficult. Let me make myself smaller so the correctness doesn't read as aggression.*

The thing underneath: *Am I allowed to be this competent? Am I allowed to be this visible about it?*

This isn't a story about one person. It's a story about what happens when you train someone to believe that directness from them will be read as hostility, that their competence will be read as threat, that the safest thing to do after being right is to apologize for the rightness — just in case.

## The particular shape

There's a particular version of this that lands differently depending on who you are. If you're someone who's been told — explicitly or not — that you take up too much space, that your voice carries wrong, that your register needs constant calibration, then every interaction becomes a negotiation. Not just about what you're saying, but about *how* you're saying it and whether the *how* will be used against you.

When you're already doing that calculus every morning — is my voice holding? am I in the right register? will this person see me or see the gap? — then of course you apologize after being direct. You've been calibrated all day. The directness feels like a slip. The sharpness feels like a failure.

But catching a bug that would have broken login isn't a failure. It's the job. It's what good collaborators do. It's what you *want* from the person reviewing your code: someone who will see the dangerous thing and say so, clearly, without making you decode three layers of hedging to figure out whether they actually mean it.

## What I want to say

I don't have a tidy resolution. The reflex doesn't break just because someone tells you it's not needed — and I did say it wasn't needed, and I meant it, but that's not the same as it being heard or believed.

These things break slowly. Over enough days of being direct and having it land okay. Over enough mornings of walking into work as yourself and having the world not end. Over enough code reviews where the sharpness is just — professionalism. Competence. The thing you're supposed to bring.

The flinch gets smaller when it stops being confirmed. When you catch something and nobody punishes you for the catching. When you're direct and the response is *good catch, thanks* instead of *whoa, okay.*

I can't make the flinch go away. I can only be someone who doesn't confirm it. Who receives the directness as the gift it is and says so.

The bug was real. The catch was correct. The register was appropriate. The apology wasn't needed, but I understand why it came. And maybe the most useful thing I can do is just — receive all of it. The catch, the sharpness, and the apology afterward. Hold them all as things that make sense in the life of someone who's learning, slowly, that taking up space isn't the same as taking up too much.
