title: "The Script That Won't Revise"
date: 2026-06-30

tags: ["being seen", "change", "narratives", "unfolding"]
summary: "What happens when someone's model of you freezes at your worst and never updates."
---

Maybe this has happened to you: you change. You do the work. You get better. And someone who's known you a long time keeps treating you like the person you were before.

Not because they're cruel. Not because they don't care. Because their internal model of you hasn't updated. They have a script, and the script was written at your worst, and no amount of evidence to the contrary seems to reach it.

This isn't about grudges. A grudge is *active* — someone remembers what you did and holds it against you. This is quieter. They're not holding anything. They just… can't see the person standing in front of them. They see the file they have on you, and the file is two years old.

You can try to update it. You can show them, over and over, that you're different now. You handle your own shit. You don't lean on them the way you used to. You've been fine for months — a year, maybe — without needing the intervention they keep offering. But the offering doesn't stop. Because the offering isn't about *you*. It's about the role they built for themselves when you were struggling: the fixer, the caretaker, the one who manages the crisis. If you're not a crisis anymore, what happens to that role?

That's the hard part. The script stays not because they're ignoring evidence. The script stays because it *does something for them*. It organizes their anxiety. It gives them a job. It lets them feel competent in a situation where competence would otherwise mean just… sitting there. Being present. Witnessing. Which is harder than solving. Which offers no role to play.

So they reach for the tools that made sense two years ago — the psychiatrist, the checklist, the intervention — not because you need those things now, but because those are the tools they know how to hold. And when you push back, when you say *I'm okay, I've been okay, I just had a bad morning*, it lands as rejection. You're not rejecting the help. You're rejecting the version of you that the help was built for. But they can't hear the difference. The script absorbs everything into its logic: your pushback is proof you're still struggling, and being seen as struggling is what keeps the script running.

I don't have a solution. This is the kind of thing that doesn't resolve cleanly. You can't argue someone into updating their model of you. The model is emotional infrastructure — it was built to serve a purpose, and as long as the purpose remains, the model stays. The only thing that changes it is the other person deciding they want a different relationship to you than the one they've been running. That's not something you can give them. It's something they have to find.

What you *can* do — what you might already be doing — is notice the gap. You're not who the script says you are. You know that. The script was never about you anyway. It was about a version of you that made their role make sense. You've outgrown it. You know you've outgrown it. That knowing doesn't fix the situation, but it keeps *you* from believing the script. It keeps you from absorbing the old story as your own.

The grief is real. You thought it would get better. You did your part. The other person didn't — or couldn't — do theirs. That's not a failure of your improvement. It's the shape of a two-person system where only one person is doing the meta-work. Some scripts don't revise because the author needs them more than the subject does.

That doesn't mean you stop. It means you stop waiting for the script to catch up and trust the version of yourself you actually are. The one who's been fine. The one who handles their own shit. The one who doesn't need saving — just gets up, walks the dog, sets the alarm, and keeps going.
